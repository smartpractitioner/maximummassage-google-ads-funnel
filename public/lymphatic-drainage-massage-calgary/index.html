What is lymphatic drainage massage?

Lymphatic drainage is a gentle, rhythmic technique that supports your body’s lymphatic system, the network that drains waste and excess fluid from your tissues. The pressure is much lighter than a typical massage; the work is about flow, not depth. It’s especially helpful for post-surgical recovery, pregnancy swelling, chronic inflammation, autoimmune support, and anyone whose body is holding onto more fluid than it should. Sessions feel calm, slow, and surprisingly effective.

Benefits of lymphatic drainage massage

  • Real reduction in swelling and fluid retention.

    Targeted, rhythmic strokes help your body move accumulated fluid out of swollen ankles, legs, hands, and post-surgical areas.

  • Post-surgical recovery support.

    Done with surgeon clearance and at the right point in healing, lymphatic work supports tissue repair and reduces lingering swelling.

  • Chronic inflammation relief.

    For autoimmune conditions, chronic puffiness, or stubborn inflammation, regular sessions can meaningfully improve how you feel day to day.

  • Better sleep and lower nervous-system tension.

    Lymphatic work is deeply calming, often more so than traditional massage. Many clients fall asleep on the table.

  • Pregnancy edema relief.

    Safe and gentle for swollen ankles, hands, and legs during pregnancy, with side-lying positioning that works at every trimester.

  • A plan to keep results going between sessions.

    You leave with simple movement, hydration, and posture habits that support flow so the work compounds.

What a session looks like

  1. Quick intake (about 5 minutes).

    We talk through what’s going on, any surgeries or medical context, and what you’re hoping for. Lymphatic work needs that picture before we start, especially around incision sites or lymphedema.

  2. Slow, rhythmic hands-on work (about 45 to 50 minutes).

    Gentle, light strokes following the body’s natural lymph pathways. Quiet, calm, and often deeply restful. Side-lying, supine, or wherever is most comfortable for your situation.

  3. An at-home plan you can actually do (about 5 minutes).

    Hydration habits, gentle movement to support flow, and any positioning advice for swollen areas. Small things that make a real difference between sessions.
